A NOTE BY SUMANTHA GHOSH

The forest is never still. Even when it feels quiet, there's movement in the undergrowth, stories in the air, and lives unfolding out of sight. That's what makes returning so rewarding — you don't step into the same jungle twice.

This edition of The Rural Explorer traces one of our most immersive journeys yet through Central India's tiger heartland. From the open meadows of Kanha to the dry bamboo slopes of Tadoba, this route holds deep memories for me — not just of wildlife, but of the communities who have lived alongside it for generations.

As part of our long-standing commitment to local stewardship, this month we've extended support to grassroots efforts around Pench and Tadoba that help provide alternative livelihoods for forest-edge communities. It's a small step in ensuring that the forests remain wild — and that those who protect them feel seen and supported.

Wherever you are reading this from, I hope this journey stirs something within — a reminder of how much the forest gives, and how quietly it asks us to give back.

— Sumantha Ghosh
Founder, The Rural Traveller

MAMMALS AND BIRDS

  • Hard Ground Barasingha – Kanha is the only place on Earth where this swamp deer thrives. Once critically endangered, their comeback is a conservation success worth witnessing.
  • Bengal Tiger - Central India offers among the highest chances to spot this elusive cat. Each park reveals a different side of the tiger's territory.
  • Indian Leopard, Dhole, Sloth Bear, Gaur, Rusty-spotted Cat - From coordinated wild dog hunts to the quiet tread of leopards at dusk, the diversity is a reminder of nature's layered complexity.
  • White-rumped, Indian & Red-headed Vultures - Critically Endangered scavengers still hold fragile ground in these parks.
  • Lesser Adjutant Stork, Grey-headed Fish Eagle, Malabar Pied Hornbill, Indian Pitta - These species bring flashes of colour and surprise to the forest canopy and river edges.

GOND & BAIGA TRADITIONS

The forests of Kanha and Pench are home to the Baiga and Gond communities—among India's oldest forest-dwelling tribes. Their myths speak of tigers as kin and forests as elders. The Gonds' forest paintings and the Baigas' intuitive knowledge of medicinal plants are woven into the ecological fabric of the land.


For travellers seeking more than sightings, time spent understanding these living traditions becomes its own kind of discovery.

SUMANTHA GHOSH

Sumantha Ghosh has spent over two decades guiding in some of India's most remarkable wild places—but few regions are as close to his heart as Central India. From the vast grasslands of Kanha to the rugged forests of Satpura, the teak woodlands of Pench, and the dry, tiger-rich zones of Tadoba, he knows these landscapes intimately.

Over the years, Sumantha has led countless nature, birding, and photography tours here for international guests, sharing stories shaped by patient observation and years in the field. His experience goes beyond guiding—during Tadoba's early rise as a wildlife destination (2008-09), he was actively involved in training local guides and building on-ground capacity, helping lay the foundation for the quality tourism Tadoba is known for today.

His calm, thoughtful approach makes every outing meaningful—whether tracking a tiger through the bamboo thickets of Pench or identifying a faraway call in Satpura's twilight. Central India is not just a destination for him—it's a long-standing relationship built on respect, learning, and deep listening.

YOGEMBER SINGH BISHT (YOGI)

For over three decades, Yogi has lived and breathed the forests of India. He began his journey in 1993 with the iconic Tiger Tops Corbett Lodge, soon becoming known for his quiet authority in the field—guiding inbound groups through Corbett and working closely with local communities under the Tiger Conservation Programme (T.C.P.C).

His field experience extends beyond Uttarakhand. From contributing to BNHS and SALCON's bird surveys to working with the Kutch Ecological Research Centre, Yogi has spent years in Gujarat's wild country, especially around endangered species like the Great Indian Bustard and Lesser Florican.

Birdwatching remains his first love. Whether tracking tigers or scanning tree canopies for hornbills, Yogi's calm presence and instinctive reading of the forest make him a guide you'll remember long after the journey ends.
